The cost and effectiveness of food aid

Unlike other countries' food aid programs, which send cash to buy food locally, the taxpayer-funded USAID Food for Peace program ships commodities grown stateside to famine-affected parts of the world. According to USAID, only 40 percent of the program's budget is spent on the food itself. The rest goes to U.S.-based processors, which are owned by agribusinesses on the Fortune 500 list, and shippers based at U.S. ports. With these ancillary interests cornering an ever increasing slice of the budget, one U.S. dollar today purchases less food and feeds far fewer people in comparison to just a decade ago.

Cost per ton to ship food

The amount of money that USAID spends to ship a ton of food has steadily risen. Two factors have contributed to a rise in costs: a law that requires shippers to use expensive U.S.-flagged vessels and a spike in global food costs since 2008.

Number of people a ton of food reaches

Africa gets the highest volume of U.S. food aid, but a ton of food reaches fewer people in Africa than in other parts of the world because of high transportation costs and a complex transit network.
